The physical sensory information of our body – such as where it is in space and how it moves – shapes much more than just our awareness of motion or position. It underlies the feeling that our body and actions truly belong to us – an experience known as embodiment. Embodiment is not only fundamental to our ability to move, learn, and retain new movements, but it is also integral to our identity, shaping how we experience ourselves and relate to others.

In turn, our interactions with the world – from simple motor actions to complex social exchanges – also shape how we perceive our body and our sense of self. This gives rise to a dynamic, reciprocal interplay between perception and action: our internal representation of our body and self both shapes and is shaped by our actions and interactions with the external environment.

I am interested in understanding this interplay across multiple levels – from lower-order processes of physical sensation and movement, to higher-order cognitive-emotional processes and social perception. In my doctoral and postdoctoral work, I studied how simple motor actions – like walking or lifting objects – and motor learning are reciprocally connected to our perception of physical body motion.

Looking ahead, I am eager to explore how basic sensorimotor processes interact with complex social dynamics to shape our experience of identity – across domains including gender, sexuality, and neurodiversity. I am particularly interested in questions such as:

  • How do our social interactions shape our perception of self, others, and our place in the world?
  • Beyond how others respond to us, how does our own social behavior and level of authenticity contribute to self-acceptance and feeling affirmed?
  • How do our emotional experiences during social interactions influence how we express our own identity and embrace identities different from our own?

The way we move is not fixed. It adapts every time our body or environment changes, like when we recover from an injury, grow taller, or simply when the ground becomes uneven. Adaptation is often automatic: we do not need to think about the details of every step and adjustment. My work explores this adaptation process:

  • How does it influence how our body feels?
  • Does it rely on cognitive resources?
  • What are the underlying mechanisms?

I study how motor, perceptual, and cognitive systems work together to keep us calibrated in the face of constant change. We often think of sensation as something that guides movement, but the relationship is reciprocal: adapting how we move also changes how those movements feel.

Why? Imagine gaining weight and adapting your gait without updating how heavy your legs feel – it would be like wearing invisible ankle weights. As we adapt, new movement patterns begin to feel “natural” again...but not entirely. Some sense of mismatch often remains. This was surprising to me: if we are adapting to a new “normal” way of moving, shouldn’t this feel normal?

In my work, I found that the new “normal” does, in fact, feel normal. But there is more to adaptation than just reestablishing what is normal, and that is why some mismatch remains.

Different learning mechanisms for different purposes

Our brain maintains an internal model of our body and environment. This guides our actions by predicting their outcomes: will stepping this way give me stability, or cause me to slip? If the prediction is wrong, the brain learns from error and recalibrates the model. My work shows that this recalibration establishes a new “normal” – a new way of moving that does also feel fully normal.

But adaptation is not just about what is normal. Walking through a crowded subway, we need to constantly adapt to short-lived disruptions – someone bumping into us, spills on the floor… This is not about updating what is normal – it is about responding quickly and flexibly to fluctuating demands. My work shows that our brain builds a flexible map of movement strategies linked to specific environmental demands. Instead of relearning each time, the brain quickly retrieves the right strategy for the situation.

Cognition, generalization, and rehabilitation

Beyond basic mechanisms, I studied real-world factors and potential applications of motor adaptation:

  • After stroke, people often have persistent limp. Is it possible that their perception of leg movement is bias? Can we recalibrate it?
  • Motor and perceptual calibrations learned in one context – like on a treadmill – do not fully carry over to other contexts – like overground. Why? Can we improve this?
  • Adaptation of walking is automatic – it does one require deliberate intention. But does this mean that it does not tax cognitive resources? And is this true as we age?
