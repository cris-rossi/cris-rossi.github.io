My Interests

The physical sensory information of our body—such as where it is in space and how it moves—shapes much more than just our awareness of motion or position. It underlies the feeling that our body and actions truly belong to us—an experience known as embodiment. Embodiment is not only fundamental to our ability to move, learn, and retain new movements, but it is also integral to our identity, shaping how we experience ourselves and relate to others.

In turn, our interactions with the world—from simple motor actions to complex social exchanges—also shape how we perceive our body and our sense of self. This gives rise to a dynamic, reciprocal interplay between perception and action: our internal representation of our body and self both shapes and is shaped by our actions and interactions with the external environment.

I am interested in understanding this interplay across multiple levels—from lower-order processes of physical sensation and movement, to higher-order cognitive-emotional processes and social perception. In my doctoral and postdoctoral work, I studied how simple motor actions—like walking or lifting objects—and motor learning are reciprocally connected to our perception of physical body motion.

Looking ahead, I am eager to explore how basic sensorimotor processes interact with complex social dynamics to shape our experience of identity—across domains including gender, sexuality, and neurodiversity. I am particularly interested in questions such as:

  • How do our social interactions shape our perception of self, others, and our place in the world?
  • Beyond how others respond to us, how does our own social behavior and level of authenticity contribute to self-acceptance and feeling affirmed?
  • How do our emotional experiences during social interactions influence how we express our own identity and embrace identities different from our own?

Current work

In my doctoral and postdoctoral research, I focused on simple interactions: walking in different environments and lifting objects. I looked at how children and adults adapt to changes in themselves and their environments by recalibrating their internal representation of their body—how it moves, how it feels, and how it engages with elements in the environment. These recalibrations can shift both perception and action: not only do we learn how to account for these changes and perform movements as intended, but these changes also start to feel normal. This process has direct implications for therapeutic interventions, as it can help in cases where our internal representation is miscalibrated.

For example, after a stroke, individuals may experience miscalibration between their legs due to hemiparesis—the weakness on one side of the body. My PhD work showed that this miscalibration can manifest in both movement and perception: individuals may limp, and that limp may feel normal. Moreover, this miscalibration can sometimes be improved using a split-belt treadmill where each leg moves at different speeds – this imposed change to the walking can sometimes help recalibrate the individual's internal representation of their legs and reduce limping.

However, this does not always happen. Why? Sometimes, our brain attributes these changes to external factors alone, rather than internalizing them as connected to our body or self. Consequently, we may not adjust our internal representations, and therapeutic interventions such as the split-belt treadmill may be less effective. My work explores how strengthening the sense that one’s actions are truly one’s own—a core aspect of embodiment—can enhance learning, retention, and the integration of new behaviors into daily life after therapeutic interventions.
